Research and Inspiration: Building on Literary Traditions

May 2, 2024 Behind the Scenes 10 min read 8,432 views 156 comments

Creating original fantasy that honors classic traditions while offering fresh perspectives requires extensive research into mythological sources and literary precedents.

The world of Doraleous and Associates didn't emerge from a vacuum. Every culture, every magical system, every heroic archetype builds upon centuries of storytelling tradition while attempting to offer something new to the fantasy landscape.

The research process began with an immersion in the foundational texts of fantasy literature. Tolkien's Middle-earth provided the template for creating internally consistent worlds where different cultures have distinct languages, histories, and values. But equally important were the works of Ursula K. Le Guin, whose Earthsea novels demonstrated how fantasy could explore complex themes of responsibility and balance.

Mining Mythological Sources

The elven Whisperwood Collective draws inspiration from Celtic and Norse traditions of sacred groves and the preservation of ancient knowledge. The idea that knowledge itself could be a living thing, requiring careful tending and protection, came from studying druidic traditions and their relationship with the natural world.

Drak Ironforge's dwarven culture incorporates elements from various mountainous societies throughout history. The concept of exile for moral principles echoes themes found in Germanic heroic literature, while the underground cities reflect both Tolkien's influence and real historical examples like the underground cities of Cappadocia.

The magical corruption that serves as the central threat draws from various mythological concepts of spiritual pollution and cosmic evil. The idea that evil could literally transform the landscape comes from sources as diverse as Celtic fairy tales and Lovecraftian cosmic horror.

Character Archetypes with Depth

Each member of the group represents a classic fantasy archetype, but with psychological complexity that goes beyond traditional roles. Doraleous as the heroic leader struggles with the burden of responsibility in ways that echo both Arthurian legend and modern leadership challenges.

Sir Walkin's paladin character draws from medieval chivalric romances but incorporates the genuine spiritual struggles of maintaining faith in the face of overwhelming darkness. His youth and inexperience make his unwavering faith both admirable and sometimes naive.

Varsuuvius represents the scholarly wizard tradition but carries the weight of historical knowledge about previous failures. This adds a tragic dimension to the wise mentor archetype, making his guidance more poignant because it comes from understanding how easily heroic quests can go wrong.

Balancing Homage and Innovation

The challenge in fantasy writing is honoring the traditions that drew you to the genre while avoiding simple imitation. Readers come to fantasy with certain expectations about how magic works, what different races represent, and how heroic quests unfold. The key is fulfilling those expectations while subverting them in meaningful ways.

The transformation of the central evil entity from destroyer to protector represents an attempt to move beyond the simple "defeat the dark lord" narrative structure. While the setup is familiar, the resolution challenges readers to consider themes of redemption and the power of compassion over destruction.

Research into various religious and philosophical traditions helped shape this approach. The concept that evil could be transformed rather than simply destroyed draws from Buddhist concepts of compassion and Christian ideas of redemption, while the practical implementation required studying various mythological examples of spiritual transformation.

Historical Research for Authenticity

Creating believable fantasy cultures requires understanding how real societies function. The political tensions between different groups in the story reflect actual historical conflicts between neighboring cultures with different values and resources.

The economic systems that support these fantasy societies needed to make sense. How do underground dwarven kingdoms obtain food? What trade relationships exist between isolated elven communities and human settlements? These questions required research into historical examples of how isolated communities maintained themselves and interacted with outsiders.

Military tactics and weapon technology also needed historical grounding. The battle sequences draw from studies of medieval warfare, but adapted for a world where magic changes the strategic landscape. Understanding how real armies moved and fought helped create more believable fantasy combat.

Language and Cultural Voice

Each culture in the story needed its own voice, reflected in dialogue patterns, naming conventions, and social structures. This required research into linguistics and anthropology to understand how language reflects cultural values.

The formal, ritualistic speech patterns of the elves reflect their long lifespans and emphasis on tradition. Dwarven dialogue tends toward directness and practicality, reflecting their craft-focused culture. Human speech varies more widely, showing the diversity within human societies.

Names carry cultural weight and needed to feel authentic to their respective societies while remaining pronounceable for readers. This meant studying naming patterns from various real cultures and adapting them for fantasy use.

The Research Never Ends

World-building is an ongoing process that continues throughout the writing. Each scene raises new questions about how the world functions, requiring additional research into everything from medieval agriculture to the psychology of leadership under extreme stress.

The goal is creating a world that feels lived-in and authentic, where readers can immerse themselves without constantly questioning the internal logic. This requires constant attention to detail and willingness to revise when research reveals better approaches.

Fantasy may be about impossible things, but it needs to be grounded in human truth to resonate with readers. The research process helps ensure that even the most fantastical elements serve meaningful purposes in exploring universal themes of courage, friendship, sacrifice, and hope.

Building on literary traditions doesn't mean being bound by them. The best fantasy honors its predecessors while forging new paths, using research and inspiration as tools for creating stories that are both familiar and surprising, traditional and innovative.
